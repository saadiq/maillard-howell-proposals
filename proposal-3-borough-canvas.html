The Challenge

New York Moves - But Not Equally

When you walk through New York City, you feel possibility everywhere but you also see the gaps.

School PE Is Shrinking

Physical education time has steadily decreased across many NYC schools, leaving students with fewer opportunities to move, play, and build lifelong healthy habits. The inequity is most visible in low-income districts.

Youth Sports Privatized

As community leagues shrink and private clubs expand, thousands of kids, especially in underserved neighborhoods, are priced out of structured sport. Access now depends more on income than interest or talent.

Seniors Lack Support

New York's older adults face barriers ranging from unsafe sidewalks to limited strength and balance programming. Many want to stay active but lack structured support for mobility, independence, and social connection.

Disability Access Limited

Adaptive programs, accessible facilities, and trained staff remain unevenly distributed across the city. Too many New Yorkers with disabilities encounter barriers that prevent full participation in movement, recreation, and sport.

The Opportunity

A Citywide Movement Framework - Beyond the Silos

Sports and recreation are often viewed as "nice to have," but they are among NYC's strongest tools for improving public health, mental wellbeing, school outcomes, workforce performance, and community safety.

The Problem: NYC's movement system is fragmented. Agencies, schools, facilities, community groups, and nonprofits all work hard, but they operate separately. The issue isn't talent or effort. It's misalignment.

The Opportunity: My vision unifies these pieces into a coordinated, citywide movement ecosystem that serves every New Yorker with clarity, consistency, and access.
